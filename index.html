Does cracking your knuckles cause arthritis?

Short answer: current evidence does not support the claim. The sound is linked to pressure changes inside joint fluid, not bones grinding together or cartilage being destroyed.

Claim Knuckle cracking causes arthritis
Verdict Not supported
Best evidence No increased hand OA risk
The myth Cracking finger joints wears them out and leads to arthritis later.
What actually pops Gas bubbles form in synovial fluid when the joint space is stretched.
Better risk factors Age, genetics, prior joint injury, weight, autoimmune disease, and overuse matter more.

One-page report

Evidence-based conclusion

01

Topic and Claim

The topic is the popular belief that cracking your knuckles causes arthritis, especially osteoarthritis in the hand. This advice is often repeated casually by family members, classmates, and online health discussions as a warning to stop the habit.

The claim sounds believable because arthritis affects joints and knuckle cracking makes a loud joint sound. However, a believable mechanism is not the same as evidence of damage.

02

Source of the Misinformation

The misinformation appears as a common health myth rather than a single original source. Harvard Health describes knuckle cracking as a long-debated habit, and JABFM notes prevalence estimates of knuckle cracking ranging from 25% to 54% depending on the population studied.

Because the habit is common and arthritis is common, the myth spreads easily through everyday advice.

03

People Potentially Affected

25-54% estimated knuckle-cracking prevalence in studied groups
53.2M U.S. adults reported diagnosed arthritis during 2019-2021
04

Scientific Evidence

Study Subjects Finding
deWeber, Olszewski & Ortolano, 2011 215 respondents, ages 50-89 Hand osteoarthritis was similar in knuckle crackers and non-crackers; duration and frequency were not linked to OA.
Castellanos & Axelrod, 1990 300 consecutive outpatients No association with arthritis was found, though the authors reported possible grip-strength differences.
Boutin et al., 2017 40 asymptomatic adults, 400 metacarpophalangeal joints No swelling, weakness, or worse hand function in habitual knuckle crackers; ultrasound visualized the cracking event.
05

Conclusion

The claim that cracking your knuckles causes arthritis is not supported by the available evidence. The best direct study on hand osteoarthritis found no higher OA rate among habitual knuckle crackers.

Cracking may still annoy people nearby, and painful or swollen joints should be checked by a clinician, but the habit itself should not be presented as a proven arthritis cause.

06

Knowledge Gaps

The research is reassuring but not perfect. More long-term prospective studies could follow younger habitual knuckle crackers for decades, measure exact cracking frequency, and separate gentle cracking from forceful joint manipulation.

Future studies could also test whether knuckle cracking affects soft tissue irritation, grip strength, or joint laxity in larger and more diverse populations.

Myth check

Cracking your knuckles does not appear to cause arthritis.

Studies comparing habitual knuckle crackers with non-crackers have not found a higher rate of hand osteoarthritis.

The pop comes from pressure changes in joint fluid, not bones grinding together.

Watch for symptoms: pain, swelling, or stiffness should be checked.
